The Howling Stars

        It was a warm June day when the rocket would launch. Allison was on it. The burning fuel pushed the behemoth of steel into the sky as a cloud of black and white smoke ballooned around it. Allison and her team were headed to a space station in the far reaches of the Earthling territory. It had been several decades since they had left Earth for good, but they were still Earthlings.

        It was called the Apollo Station. Allison vaguely remembered a story about a different Apollo mission, but she couldn’t gather any specific details. As they left the atmosphere of their planet behind, the mission commander— Gerald Lylan— spoke over the roar of the engines.

        “We have left the planet behind. We are not coming back for a long time. I hope you said your good-byes.”

        Allison had. She had bid farewell to her family and friends the night before. Now she dearly missed them.

        “Anyway,” Lylan continued. “When we get to Apollo, the existing team will show us around. We have already been assigned positions, so the integration should be smooth.”

        On a signal from the pilot, Lylan sat back down. It must be time for the cambyon jump, Allison reckoned. Soon before the egress from Earth, a physicist had discovered a type of phase-changing particle, capable of switching between bradyon and tachyon form. It was really ingenious, she had been told. She didn’t entirely understand them, but no one else did either, so it was alright. She was an astrobiologist, not a rocket scientist.

        The pilot pulled a lever back. For a brief moment, nothing happened. Then, the stars blinked to a different position. One star was very bright, about the size of the sun viewed from their home planet.

        Apollo was orbiting it. The team could see it transiting across that star.

        The pilot radioed to the station. “This is Echo-Sierra-Alpha-Nine-Eight. Apollo Station, do you read me? Over.”

        “This is Apollo Station. We hear you. Over.”

        “Apollo Station, permission to land?”

        After that, it dissolved to pilot jargon about where to land, transmissions of telemetry. Eventually, they docked and the new team— there were five of them, counting Allison and Lylan— exited the spacecraft. Behind them, the five oldest members of Apollo’s crew got onto the ship. They would help unload some supplies, then they would leave. It worked like this every crew rotation. In six rotations, Allison would leave. It was a thirty-five person crew. The rotations came every few months.

        Communication with home was impossible in any reasonable window because of how far out they were. The radio communications would take at least a year and a half to go one way. They also couldn’t send cambyonic signals— it was too expensive to make regular jumps. Instead, crewmembers send letters back with the leaving crewmembers. There were some letters in the cargo that was currently being unloaded.

        One of the existing crewmembers walked over to Allison. “Dr. Katerina?”

        Allison turned to him. “Yes?”

        He smiled and put his hand out. She shook it. “What’s your name?”

        “Eli Braden. I’m the resident agent. I hear you’re the astrobiologist.”

        “That’s correct.”

        “I’ll show you around, then.” Eli walked away, Allison following closely. As they left the flurry of motion around the landing craft, they came to a large window. Through it, Allison could see the star. It was green.

        It was beautiful. Through the tinted windows, she could’ve stared at it for hours. It was a green glass sea, light and heat swimming in a symphony of stardust.

        “That’s why I became an astronomer,” Eli said. “The stars enchanted me.”

        Allison nodded and looked at it a while longer. Eli continued. “We call it the Howling Star. Or the Howler. Or whatever.”

        Someone ran up to Eli. “We found another one.”

        Eli turned. “Where is it this time, John?”

        “The hallway connecting Cells A and B. It’s still only on the A side.” The Apollo station was separated into five cells, with seven staff assigned to each cell. Allison remembered reading about this design in her manuals. The cells, large building-sized facilities, were attached only superficially, with doors locked on both sides. If ever the Apollo station needed more cells, they could be easily attached. Each cell had four attaching points.

        Eli turned to Allison, “I need to look at this before we can get to your assignment. It’s on the way, I promise.”

        The hallway was relatively short. The doors were only about a meter and a half from the beginning of the hallway. Allison realized what John had been talking about. Long, deep marks were gouged into the shiny metal. Eli knelt down and brushed them.

        “This is not the first time this has happened.” Eli shook his head. “It’s alway in Cell A. We have no idea what’s going on.” Eli slumped down and leaned against the scratches. “I’m the manager on this station. I’m supposed to know how to fix this. I just can’t figure it out. Everyone has a solid alibi. Far as I can tell, no one is doing this to the station.”

        Allison frowned. “Well, I’m just an astrobiologist, not a detective, but I’ll keep an eye out.” Allison’s parents, though, had been detectives. She had worked at their agency for a few years, before she pursued her graduate degree, so she knew a lot of things about detectivery. Enough to know that wasn’t a word.

        Identify the suspects. Analyze data. Form storylines. Fact check. Repeat. Those were the steps she followed. She didn’t know about others, but it had always worked for her.

        Allison helped Eli up. “Will you take me to my assignment now?”

        Eli nodded and started walking. She caught up. “What exactly is your job? You said you were an agent, but what does that mean?”

        Eli nodded again. “I work for the government.” He paused. “Well, we all do, but I don’t for the space agency. I’m kinda the manager of the station and, more specifically, for this cell. I stay on longer than anyone else, just to provide a kind of stability. You could say I’m in charge, but I don’t really tell anyone what to do.”

        Eli stopped beside a door. He rapped his knuckles on it, then scanned his ID card. The doors swung open. Inside, strands of plants hung from the ceiling up.

        “Be aware, this room does not have the magnetic gravity.” In the rest of the station, a slight electromagnetic field was being produced, simulating the normal gravity of their new home. Metal strips in the spacefarers’ shoes and on their epaulets kept them tethered down. In this plant room, the field was turned off, allowing for the normal microgravity of orbit to take effect.

        Allison stepped into the room. She felt the magnetic gravity disappear behind her. The force of her step propelled her up, into the plants. All around her, the dark vivacious green enchanted her, reminding her of simpler days, years ago, running through the woods with her best friends…

        She floated through the forest, just remembering.

        Allison collided with someone else. They grunted in surprise. They turned. The person was a slender woman wearing glasses. Her hair floated freely behind her. If Allison’s hadn’t been done up in a bun, she assumed that hers would be the same.

        “Hello!” the woman said cheerfully. “You must be Allison!”

        “I am, yes. What’s your name?”

        The woman smiled. “Lexy. Alexandra Sheila Donovan, if we’re being fancy.”

        “Are you a biologist?” Allison asked.

Lexy smiled and spun around. “Yes. I just love plants!”

“I can tell.” Allison was honestly impressed at Lexy’s demeanor. She was very cheerful and carefree. Allison had never been able to maintain that. Something was always going wrong, it seemed.

Lexy’s smile grew larger, somehow. “Are you?”

“What?”

“An astrobiologist?”

“Oh.” Allison nodded. “Yeah. Why did you think I was in here?”

“I thought you might just be visiting the plants. I would.”

Allison smiled, trying to reflect the energy that Lexy was showing to her. Allison’s mind was somewhere else. It was on the vandalism. Allison really wanted to investigate. But one of her skills, useful as a graduate student and now as a practicing astrobiologist, was focus. Indomitable will and focus. She cleared her mind of the vandalism and focused on the plants.

“Lexy, will you show me around? I want to start working soon.”

Lexy beamed. “Of course!”

***

        Eli Braden looked at the scratches. After dropping Dr. Katerina off, he had returned to the scene of the crime. It really wasn’t any of the crew. They all had solid alibis. Security recordings proved that.

        Allison Katerina. The name had been nagging at him since he heard it.

        He slapped his forehead.

        Allison ‘Eyes’ Katerina. Her parents’ star detective. The one that solved his brother’s murder. His brother was in jail now, where he belonged.

        John, the one that had informed him of the newest vandalism— he was a mathematician, calculating orbital injections and the like, turned the corner and walked toward Eli. “Hey, chief. You’ve got a letter here.”

        “Thanks, bud.” Eli took the letter. He opened it and began to read.

        Dear Elias,

        I figure I should be the one to tell you. Marie died yesterday. It was my fault, really. I was the one that stabbed her, all those years ago. My job is finally done. Her brothers died then; I’ve been waiting to complete the set. I know you loved her and I know how much this hurts you.

        That was the plan.

        In any case,

        Ebenezer

        Eli leaned against the wall. He slid down and hung his head.

        “It finally happened,” he whispered. “She finally died. Ben got her.”

        Ebenezer was his murderer brother. He had attempted to kill Eli’s fiancee and family. He had killed her four brothers, but had only fatally wounded Marie. Her sister hadn’t been there. Marie had been in a coma for several years.

        And now she was gone.

        What did anything matter. Anymore.

***

        Allison floated toward the exit. As she stepped through the gateway, the magnetic field pulled her down to the floor. Lexy followed behind her.

        Lexy led them to the mess hall, where they enjoyed— enjoyed would be a strong term; space food is rather bland— a filling meal. After that, they headed to bed. Lexy showed Allison to hers, then left to get to her own.

        Allison woke in the middle of the night. Something deep inside her was shaking. Some violent memory of a day long ago, something from her days at the agency. The memories of stalking some criminal or another catching up to her. She sat on the side of her bed. Images of angry men and women rolled through her mind. Allison stood up and shook her head, trying to clear it. She pushed open her door and walked briskly through the hallway.

        She walked to the main atrium— where the window showed the Howling Star in the distance. Allison looked out at the star again. Despite its name, it had a calming effect on the former detective. The bright burning reminded her of the forest. The green light was enchanting. Intellectually, Allison knew that the star wasn’t really green. There was a coating on the window blocking higher frequencies of the light. That was also why she could look directly at it. It didn’t really matter to her, though. The light was hers to see.

        After a few minutes of gazing quietly at the Howler, Allison continued to walk around the cell. This cell was the newest one. It had the ship docking area, as well as the plant room. It had a few sleeping quarters, but nothing else. The mess hall where she and Lexy had had dinner the night before was in a different cell. Allison wasn’t sure of the layout of the entire station yet. Deep in thought, Allison accidentally ran into someone.

        “Sorry,” Allison said. She looked at the person. It was Eli. “Hey, Eli. Why are you up?”

        Eli looked around. “I couldn’t sleep. You?”

        She nodded. “Same.”

        Eli took a deep breath. “I found some more vandalism. I was hoping I could use some of that Eyes Katerina skill.”

        Allison took a step back. “H-how? How do you know who I was?”

        “You caught a murderer. I knew hi— his victims.”

        She nodded. “Okay. Take me to it.”

***

        Eli pushed open a door. It opened into a closet. Before he opened it all the way, he paused.

        “This closet is used for storing some chemicals Alexandra is using on the plants. It’s a whole thing. She’ll explain it, probably.”

        He opened it fully.

        Five deep gashes reached around all the walls. Shattered bottles lay on the floor, their non-magnetic contents floating through the air. The brightly colored liquids danced like the flows in the Howling Star.

        Allison looked at Eli.        “When did this happen?”

        “Recently. Alexandra popped in here right before I sealed the cells; she would’ve mentioned something. It had to have been tonight.”

        Allison looked at Eli. “What do you mean, ‘seal the cells’?”

        Eli looked at the station’s scars. “I lock the doors between the cells. Each one is fully independent at night. In case something goes wrong in one cell, it won’t transfer around. Kinda silly, if you aske me, but protocol is protocol.”

        Allison nodded. If the cells were separated, then the vandal had to be someone that slept in this cell. That limited the amount of suspects significantly.

        “Who all is quartered in this cell?”

        Eli counted on his fingers. “You and I, Alexandra Donovan, Gerald Lylan, Qi Asimov, and Christian Holt. You and Lylan just arrived.” He laughed. “You know that. It can’t be either of you; this has been going on too long for that. That leaves Asimov, Alexandra, or Christian.”

        “I haven’t met Asimov or Holt yet. Where do they work?”

        “Holt’s a physicist. His grandpa discovered cambyons, actually. Anyway, he’s a physicist. He works with John and I, over in Cell C. I’ve talked to him about this before. He’s had a solid alibi, far as I could tell. You could try talking to him again, though.”

        “What about Asimov?”

        “He’s a science fiction author.”

        Allison looked shocked. “What?”

        Eli laughed. “No. Isaac Asimov was an author, back in the twentieth century. It was a joke. Asimov is a geologist. He works on our terraforming project. He’s in Cell D.”

        “I’ll talk to them tomorrow, if I can get Lexy to give me time off. Why do you call her Alexandra, by the way?”

        Eli looked around, uncomfortably. “We used to be friends, but I made some mistakes.” He shrugged. “She doesn’t talk to me anymore. I stopped calling her Lexy. It just felt wrong. Don’t ask her about it, please. It would only make her sad.”

***

        Allison floated next to Lexy.

        “So, how’d you sleep last night?” Allison asked.

        “Like an insomniac that takes sleeping pills,” Lexy flashed a smile.

        “Do you?” Allison asked. “Take sleeping pills, I mean.”

        “No. I just meant I slept really well.”

        “Oh,” Allison said.

Lexy started filling a spray bottle.

        “What’s that for?” Allison asked.

        “It’s a fungicide. A few months ago, we had someone come up, but they hadn’t properly quarantined and they brought up an annoying fungus.”

        “Huh.”

        “This is heavily diluted. Our concentrated version is toxic to most organisms. Causes intense pain, and the like.” Lexy handed Allison a black plastic bottle with a danger symbol on it, then flew off to apply the fungicide.

        Allison didn’t even study plants. She had no idea what she was doing here.

        Immediately after that, Eli floated up beside her.

        “Allison, can I borrow you for a minute?”

        Allison looked over and down at Lexy. She was looking intensely at her work, with a grim look on her face, distinctly unlike the Lexy Allison knew.

        Allison nodded to Eli. “Sure.”

        The duo pushed off the workstation, flying through the green plants, arriving at the gateway. The magnetic gravity took hold of them and they began to walk.

        “What do you need to talk about?” Allison asked.

        “I remembered we have cameras, scattered about the station. We can check the tapes.”

        “Oh. Why are there cameras?”

        “Great question. I think something about an immortal record of our science.”

        “Mm.” Allison hummed. “Where are the tapes?”

        “I have them in my office. Ah! Here we are.”

        Eli pushed open the door to his office. He sat down at his desk and pulled up the files on his computer. He scrolled to the right camera and played the clip.

        For a few minutes, the camera is still and silent on the door to the storage closet. Then, Lexy opens the door. She walks inside.

        Eli switched to the camera inside the closet.

        Lexy rummages around, looking for something. She pulls out a bottle, then walks out.

        Eli paused the video. “I was around for this part. After she took you to your room, she found that chemical, took it to the plant lab, then she went to her room. I was locking the doors at the time. That’s why I saw this.” He unpaused the video.

        For a long moment, nothing happens. As Eli switches the playback to a higher speed, the screen ripples. Then, a dark figure rushes in and out of the closet.

        “There!” Eli said. He went back and played the video at normal speed.

This darkened figure walks slowly into frame. Their gait is halting and unsteady. It pushes open the closet door.

Eli switched to the inside camera again.

The large figure raises a hand. Giant claws extend, protruding from its fingers. It slashes across the room, shattering bottles and carving deeply into the wall. Just as it arrived, the figure walks out of the closet.

“Where did it come from?” Allison asked. Eli put the video in reverse and followed the vandal as it moved back in time. It stalked down the hallways in reverse, eventually entering a lavatory.

“Sneaky devil,” Eli whispered. “We don’t have cameras in there.”

“Well, let’s take a look around in there,” Allison suggested.

“You can,” Eli suggested. “It’s a women’s room. I’ll stay here and look over the footage from other incidents.”

“All right.”

Allison walked out of Eli’s office.

***

        Eli scrolled back through the timeline. All the recorded incidents were like the one in the supply closet. The attacker entered an area, then damages it. Eli started plotting the locations of the attacks on a map of the station. He couldn’t see any pattern. That didn’t mean there wasn’t one; he just couldn’t see it. This was the issue. He didn’t have a lick of crime-solving ability. Not like Allison. She could do anything. Even his suggestion of the security cameras just came from a novel he had been reading.

        Eyes Katerina had caught his brother. His brother had killed his fiancée. His fiancée had a sister, the only living sibling. That sibling lived on the station and was friends with Allison.

        Alexandra.

        That was why he didn’t call her Lexy. He’d gotten Marie killed.

        Eli screamed silently, again feeling the sharp loss of the only person he’d cared about.

        He missed her.

        So he worked. To numb his pain.

***

        Allison walked back into the plant lab. She soared up to the workbench. After she had inspected the bathroom— there hadn’t been anything there— she had reported to Eli. With no other leads, she returned to the lab. Lexy wasn’t at the bench. She was probably out doing something with the plants.

        Allison looked about and spotted her. Allison pushed off and went down to where Lexy was.

        “What are we doing here?” Allison smiled.

        Lexy looked up and smiled and began to explain.

***

        Allison slept peacefully that night. But when she awoke, she was met with a storm of confusion.

        “He’s dead, Elias!” someone thundered. His voice carried even through the walls, into Allison’s room.

        Allison quickly dressed as they continued to argue. Their voices dropped lower, falling out of her ears. She rushed into the crowd. Most of the crew was there. When she saw what they were gathered around, she gasped.

        “Gerald?”

        Gerald Lylan was dead. His body lay cold and dead, slashes across his chest. His torso was shredded, dried blood pooled on the floor. His death wounds were the same pattern as all the other vandalisms.

        This just got very real.

        Someone pushed past Allison with a sheet. They wrapped Lylan’s body.

        “Y’all can argue later. It is not right to disrespect the dead.”

        The man that had yelled at Eli looked at the one that had wrapped Lylan. “Thank you, Qi.”

        So that was Qi Asimov. He seemed respectable.

Eli dropped to the floor, crouching.

“This is a tragedy, I agree. But there’s nothing we can do. We need to continue our work. I will find the murderer.”

“Like you found the vandal?” the man said incredulously.

“That’s enough, Christian!” Eli said. “This is going to change things. Allison there was a professional detective a few years ago. She’ll find him.”

Allison’s eyes widened at the implication of that. She didn’t know if she could do this as quickly as needed. But she would try her best.

“I can do it,” Allison said. For some reason, that seemed to calm the crowd and they began to disperse.

Allison walked over to Eli, who was still crouching on the floor.

“I have an idea,” he said. “I’m going to write a program to monitor the camera feeds. It will alert us both when the murderer shows up again.”

“Okay,” Allison said. Eli jumped up and ran off.

Allison stood motionless for a second, then walked over to the plant lab.

***

        That evening, an alarm blared in Allison’s room. For a moment, she was utterly confused, but then she remembered Eli’s program.

        That meant that the monster was back.

        Allison ran out of her room. She didn’t know where it was. She ran around the outside of the central block of rooms. She would go to that bathroom and—

She stopped dead in her pathway. In front of the window, there stood the beast.

        It was several feet taller than an average person. It hunched its back, to avoid hitting the ceiling. Its arms hung down, ending in giant claws. The monster turned and looked at Allison.

        “Hello, Ally,” it growled. Its voice sounded like nails on a chalkboard. “I expected you would get this far. I didn’t think it would take this long, though.”

        Ally yelled at it. “You killed Gerald!”

        It cocked its head. “I suppose I did. Would you like to watch this time?”

        It raised its clenched fist. A struggling figure pushed against their captor.

        Lexy.

        Lexy screamed for Allison to get away, but she refused. Allison rushed toward the monster and its captive astrobiologist. With a single swipe from the murderer, Allison was thrown back twenty or thirty feet, landing right next to the door to the plant lab with a sickening crunch and a wave of pain.s

        A sudden flash of inspiration came to her.

        She stood, the intense pain of a broken rib nearly pushing her down. Her brain shot her full of adrenaline and she crashed through the door. She rocketed up to the workstation and grabbed the fungicide.

        She pushed back down to the gateway. When the magnetic gravity grabbed her, the inexpressably intense pain returned. She collapsed to the floor. The monster was drawing closer.

        “Ally, as an astrobiologist, I thought you would’ve figured it out by now.”

        The murderer was only a few yards away now.

        “How does first contact feel?”

        Allison loosened the cap of the fungicide, then threw the bottle with the force and accuracy of an All-American baseball player. The non-magnetic bottle and liquid soared without descent, smacking the monster squarely in the face. The loosened cap dislodged, spraying the liquid in its face.

        It shrieked, pulling its hands up to its head. As it did so, it dropped Lexy. She dashed over to Allison.

        “Quick thinking,” Lexy said.

        The monster collapsed, shaking the floor.

        Allison groaned from the pain. She and Lexy looked over at the monster. It seized a few times, then fell still. Its dull black skin grew shiny. It dissolved, a tarrish liquid flowing across the station floor.

        But still, there was a body in the center of the liquid. The person was breathing heavily, lying facedown.

        Lexy walked up to it. She flipped it over, gasping when she saw his face.

        Eli Braden was the monster.
